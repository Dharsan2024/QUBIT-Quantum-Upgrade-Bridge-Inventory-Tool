Advanced Cryptographic Agility and Risk Quantification: Orchestrating Post-Quantum Migration Frameworks
The Existential Threat of Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computers
The proliferation of quantum computing research has rapidly accelerated the timeline toward the realization of a Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC). Such a machine poses a systemic and existential threat to the foundational mathematics underpinning modern global cybersecurity protocols. Specifically, the security of current asymmetric cryptographic systems—including the widely deployed RSA, Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH), and the Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA)—relies on the computational intractability of integer factorization and the discrete logarithm problem for classical computing architectures1. A CRQC, leveraging Shor's algorithm, can solve these exact mathematical problems in polynomial time, rendering currently deployed public-key infrastructure effectively obsolete and entirely defenseless1.
While a fault-tolerant CRQC of sufficient scale does not exist today, the urgency of this threat is governed by the phenomenon of "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" (HNDL) operations. Nation-state adversaries and sophisticated threat actors are systematically intercepting and storing massive volumes of encrypted network traffic in cold storage1. Their objective is to warehouse highly sensitive data—such as classified government communications, proprietary intellectual property, sealed legal records, and longitudinal medical histories—until quantum hardware matures to the point where this archived ciphertext can be retroactively decrypted1. Because data sensitivity often spans decades, the cryptographic vulnerability exists today, irrespective of the exact year quantum advantage is achieved. The necessity for immediate intervention is formalized by Mosca’s Theorem, which quantifies the urgency of post-quantum migration through a temporal inequality:   1. In this equation,    represents the required security shelf-life of the data,    denotes the time required to inventory and migrate the cryptographic infrastructure, and    represents the probabilistic time remaining until a CRQC becomes operational1. If the combined duration of the data's required secrecy and the migration effort exceeds the time before quantum maturity, the system is already in a state of critical compromise.
Despite urgent advisories from regulatory and intelligence bodies, including the National Security Agency (NSA) through its Commercial National Security Algorithm Suite 2.0 (CNSA 2.0) and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), organizations face systemic paralysis1. The industry suffers from a profound lack of visibility into its own encrypted communications, resulting in an inability to quantify which assets are actively exposed to HNDL threats1. Furthermore, the market lacks open-source, automated migration tooling capable of discovering cryptographic assets, prioritizing their remediation through probabilistic risk models, and executing the transition to the algorithmic standards defined by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)1. The resulting gap between theoretical guidance and operational execution necessitates the development of a comprehensive, open-source HNDL risk quantification engine and automated Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) migration orchestrator.
Theoretical Foundations and Base Literature Analysis
To construct a robust migration framework, it is imperative to ground the system's architecture in peer-reviewed literature encompassing both quantum hardware scaling and cryptographic static analysis. The proposed project leverages two distinct domains of academic research to form its theoretical scaffolding: quantum timeline hardware simulation and cryptographic API misuse detection.
Quantum Hardware Scaling and the Timeline to Advantage
Estimating the variable    in Mosca's Theorem requires transitioning away from binary assumptions—that a quantum computer simply will or will not arrive by a certain date—and moving toward probabilistic Monte Carlo simulations fueled by empirical hardware specifications1. The foundational text for this component is the 2022 research by Webber, Elfving, Weidt, and Hensinger, published in AVS Quantum Science, which investigates the impact of hardware specifications on reaching quantum advantage in the fault-tolerant regime2.
Webber et al. demonstrate that variations in quantum hardware specifications, particularly code cycle times and the quality of qubit control, drastically impact the physical qubit overhead required to execute Shor's algorithm and other complex quantum circuits2. Crucially, the researchers established that slower code cycle times in certain quantum architectures can be mathematically mitigated by introducing additional physical qubits2. By employing advanced space and time optimization strategies previously developed for error-correcting surface codes—specifically comparing parallelization methods like the Game of Surface Code's Units and AutoCCZ factories—Webber et al. proved that computations can be incrementally accelerated until a fundamental reaction-limited rate is reached2.
The most actionable data from this research, which serves as the statistical foundation for the proposed framework's Monte Carlo simulations, is the estimation of resources required to break 256-bit elliptic curve encryption (specifically the ECDSA protocol utilized by the Bitcoin network)2. Webber’s calculations indicate that breaking this encryption within a one-hour timeframe requires approximately 317 million physical qubits, assuming a surface code cycle time of 1 microsecond, a reaction time of 10 microseconds, and a physical gate error rate of   2. However, if the decryption window is relaxed to one day, the requirement drops precipitously to just 13 million physical qubits8. Furthermore, the researchers noted that the physical footprint of such a trapped-ion quantum computer has been theoretically reduced from the size of a football pitch (  ) to a mere    due to architectural advancements8. By extracting these precise qubit, gate depth, and temporal thresholds, the proposed risk quantification engine can accurately map specific legacy key sizes (e.g., RSA-2048) to the probable timeline of quantum capability.
Cryptographic API Discovery and Static Analysis
The second foundational pillar addresses the variable    in Mosca’s Theorem: the discovery and migration effort. Historically, developers have demonstrated a systemic inability to implement cryptographic APIs securely, often defaulting to deprecated algorithms, insufficient key lengths, or insecure initialization vectors10. The base literature for analyzing this phenomenon is centered around the development of CrySL and the CogniCrypt static analysis engine, pioneered by researchers such as Bodden and Krüger10.
CrySL was engineered as a domain-specific specification language designed to bridge the cognitive gap between cryptography experts and software engineers by allowing experts to formalize the secure usage parameters of cryptographic libraries10. The CogniCrypt engine translates these CrySL specifications into context-sensitive and flow-sensitive demand-driven static data-flow analyses12. When CogniCrypt was deployed against a dataset of 10,000 Android applications and over 200,000 Java software artifacts on Maven Central, the results were catastrophic: 95% of Android applications and over 63% of Java artifacts contained at least one severe cryptographic misuse11.
While CogniCrypt and competing tools like CryptoGuard (which utilizes demand-driven program slicing) represent the vanguard of traditional static analysis, recent literature indicates their limitations11. Traditional static application security testing (SAST) tools rely on rigid, hard-coded rulesets that often fail in edge cases involving complex string manipulations or obfuscated abstractions (e.g., failing to flag the deprecated DES cipher when parameterized as "des" instead of "DES")15. Recent comparative studies have demonstrated that Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4o-mini, frequently surpass state-of-the-art static tools in detecting complex cryptographic misuses across benchmark datasets like CryptoAPI and MASC because of their ability to understand semantic context rather than just abstract syntax15. The proposed framework integrates this exact evolution in the literature, utilizing Tree-sitter for the initial Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) parsing, but ultimately deploying localized LLMs to interpret the cryptographic context and generate automated code refactoring diffs1.

CycloneDX and the Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM)
A critical outcome of the discovery phase is the generation of standardized documentation that satisfies emerging government mandates, such as the U.S. White House memorandum on PQC migration and NIST SP 1800-38B5. The proposed framework fulfills this requirement by automatically generating a Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM) using the CycloneDX specification (ECMA-424)19.
A CBOM is a structured, machine-readable object model that provides exhaustive transparency into the cryptographic assets utilized within a software supply chain, detailing algorithms, keys, certificates, and protocols19. The introduction of the Cryptography Registry in CycloneDX v1.7 resolved historical challenges related to the inconsistent naming of algorithms across different vendor tools, which previously hindered cross-organizational PQC readiness assessments23.
By exporting JSON or XML files compliant with CycloneDX v1.7 schemas, the framework categorizes assets using highly specific property fields. For instance, a discovered network protocol is defined using the assetType (e.g., protocol), the specific type (e.g., tls or ssh), the version (e.g., TLSv1.2), and an enumeration of the cipherSuites and algorithms in use24. When cataloging cryptographic keys, the framework records the state (e.g., active, suspended, destroyed, aligning with NIST SP 800-57 lifecycles), the size in bits, the algorithmRef, and the securedBy parameter to denote whether the key is protected in software, a Hardware Security Module (HSM), or a Trusted Platform Module (TPM)20.
Furthermore, the CycloneDX standard utilizes precise algorithm patterns to eliminate ambiguity. For example, the pattern RSA-PKCS1-1.5[-{digestAlgorithm}][-{keyLength}] allows the framework to explicitly document an asset as RSA-PKCS1-1.5-SHA-256-2048, ensuring that security analysts and compliance officers possess a granular, mathematically precise inventory of their exposure23. This CBOM generation not only facilitates immediate risk assessment but also integrates seamlessly with Vulnerability Exploitability eXchange (VEX) data, allowing organizations to instantly determine if a newly discovered vulnerability in a specific TLS library impacts their compiled artifacts19.
The HNDL Risk Quantification Engine Architecture
The intellectual core of the framework is the HNDL Risk Quantifier, which abandons binary vulnerability checklists in favor of continuous, probabilistic modeling1. Doing everything equally in cybersecurity equates to doing nothing; organizations require a mathematical model that dictates which cryptographic asset to migrate first1.
This prioritization is achieved through a multi-tiered AI and statistical pipeline. The initial stage involves a Data Sensitivity Classifier—a fine-tuned BERT or DistilBERT model—that analyzes the surrounding semantic context of a discovered cryptographic asset1. By reading source code comments, variable naming conventions, and API endpoints, the model classifies the underlying data (e.g., into financial records, intellectual property, or ephemeral session tokens) and assigns a required secrecy shelf-life (the variable    in Mosca's Theorem)1.
Simultaneously, the Quantum Timeline Simulator constructs a probability distribution for the arrival of a CRQC16. Using the pgmpy Bayesian network library and SciPy, the system executes Monte Carlo simulations over the hardware specifications established by Webber et al. (such as error rates, physical qubit availability, and code cycle times)1. This engine maps the specific algorithm and key size (e.g., RSA-2048 versus AES-128) to the computational complexity required by Shor's or Grover's algorithms to execute a successful break1.
These disparate data streams converge within a Gradient Boosted (XGBoost) risk score regressor1. The regressor calculates the specific probability that the intercepted ciphertext will be successfully decrypted by a quantum adversary before the data's sensitivity classification dictates it is obsolete16. The output is a highly nuanced Risk Score, normalized between 0 and 1, presented alongside a statistical confidence interval, which directly dictates the operational priority queue for the Migration Orchestrator16.
Automated Remediation and the Hybrid Key Exchange Bridge
Once the risk queue is populated, the Migration Orchestrator executes the automated transition to post-quantum standards16. A NetworkX Graph Neural Network analyzes the source code to identify intricate crypto dependency chains, ensuring that fundamental key generation utilities are migrated prior to the signature verification functions that rely upon them1.
The actual code transformation is powered by a local Ollama LLM, ensuring that proprietary enterprise source code is never exfiltrated to public cloud AI APIs1. The LLM ingests the vulnerable abstract syntax tree and generates precise code diffs, systematically swapping legacy Java Cryptography Architecture or C-based OpenSSL calls with their post-quantum counterparts, such as transitioning RSA to the Module-Lattice-Based Key-Encapsulation Mechanism (ML-KEM) or transitioning ECDSA to the Module-Lattice-Based Digital Signature Algorithm (ML-DSA)1.
However, enforcing a hard cutover from classical to post-quantum cryptography in a production environment introduces catastrophic operational fragility, as legacy clients may be unable to negotiate the new protocols. Furthermore, the newly standardized lattice-based algorithms have not withstood decades of classical cryptanalysis, meaning a mathematical flaw could theoretically be discovered, rendering them vulnerable to standard classical computers25.
To mitigate this, the framework implements a Dual-Stack Hybrid Bridge1. Utilizing the liboqs library and the oqs-provider for OpenSSL, this bridge configures the application to execute a hybrid key exchange1. As defined in IETF TLS 1.3 hybrid design drafts, this mechanism utilizes multiple key exchange algorithms simultaneously (e.g., X25519 combined with ML-KEM-768) and concatenates the resulting secrets6. This ensures that the overall communication remains secure even if one of the component algorithms is completely compromised25. The framework's Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) Patch Generator automatically outputs Terraform or Ansible scripts to configure this hybrid posture at the ingress controllers (Nginx, Apache), optimizing for low latency and high performance to prevent the larger post-quantum ciphertexts from causing severe network degradation16.
Strategy for Evaluation Committee Presentation and Demonstration
Presenting an architectural framework of this magnitude to an academic guide and an evaluation committee requires a carefully orchestrated demonstration that proves the software's operational efficacy beyond static diagrams. The objective is to simulate a real-world HNDL attack vector and systematically demonstrate how the framework discovers, quantifies, and remediates the vulnerability16. The presentation should be structured into four sequential, interactive phases.
The first phase establishes the baseline vulnerability. The presenter will initialize a mock enterprise application that actively relies on RSA-2048 and ECDH for its TLS communications. Utilizing a packet capture tool such as Wireshark or tcpdump, the presenter will simulate the adversary's perspective in an HNDL operation, capturing and saving the encrypted ciphertext as it traverses the network16. This physically demonstrates the ease with which present-day data is warehoused for future quantum decryption.
The second phase transitions to the Discovery and Inventory Layer. The presenter will execute the framework against the mock application's source code repository16. The committee will observe the terminal output as the Tree-sitter AST parser rapidly identifies the exact file paths, line numbers, and legacy cryptographic APIs in use across multiple languages1. The presenter will then showcase the automated generation of the CycloneDX v1.7 CBOM, proving that the tool natively outputs machine-readable JSON files compliant with international standard ECMA-424, a critical requirement for modern IT governance19.
The third phase involves the visualization of the HNDL Risk Quantifier. The presenter will open the interactive Plotly/Dash UI, which displays the enterprise risk posture1. The dashboard will feature the Bayesian network's output, visualizing the intersection of the data sensitivity lifetime against the Monte Carlo simulation of the CRQC arrival timeline16. By demonstrating how the XGBoost regressor prioritizes the discovered assets based on their probabilistic risk of decryption prior to obsolescence, the presenter provides the committee with tangible proof of the AI components' analytical depth1.
The fourth and final phase is the empirical remediation. The presenter will trigger the Migration Orchestrator, allowing the committee to watch the local Ollama LLM autonomously generate the exact code diffs required to replace the vulnerable APIs1. Following the automated patching and compilation of the source code, the presenter will re-initiate the network traffic. A subsequent Wireshark packet capture will definitively prove that the application is now successfully negotiating a hybrid post-quantum TLS key exchange (e.g., X25519 + Kyber768)26. This end-to-end execution irrevocably transitions the project from a theoretical proposal to a verifiable, enterprise-ready security platform16.

The Academic Vanguard: IIT Madras and Lattice-Based Cryptography
At the academic forefront of the Chennai ecosystem is the research led by Professor Shweta Agrawal within the Department of Computer Science and Engineering at IIT Madras35. Professor Agrawal, a recipient of the prestigious Swarnajayanti Fellowship and the leader of the Arvind Raghunathan Center for Theoretical CS (aRtCS), specializes in post-quantum cryptography, specifically focusing on the mathematical structures underlying lattice-based encryption36.
Lattice-based cryptography forms the mathematical foundation for the primary NIST post-quantum standards (such as ML-KEM and ML-DSA) because it relies on the extreme computational hardness of solving specific geometric problems in high-dimensional spaces—problems that are not known to offer any algorithmic speedups to quantum computers38. As the dimensions of the lattice scale into the hundreds or thousands, calculating this shortest vector or solving the related Learning With Errors (LWE) problem becomes theoretically intractable for both classical supercomputers and CRQCs35.
The research output from IIT Madras extends far beyond foundational encryption into highly advanced theoretical capabilities, including secure distributed computing, Byzantine fault tolerance, computing on encrypted data, and the provable security of asymmetric primitives38. The threat is not a distant theoretical possibility; the reality of Harvest Now, Decrypt Later operations dictates that the cryptographic vulnerabilities present in today's networks will inevitably translate into the catastrophic data breaches of the next decade1. The proposed architectural framework successfully bridges the chasm between theoretical quantum risk quantification and practical, automated software engineering.
